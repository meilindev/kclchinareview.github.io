From Asymmetric Partnership to Managed Rivalry: The Economic Interdependence and Strategic Competition Between Germany and China

By Mariam Benamer | 13th April 2026

When German Chancellor Friedrich Merz spoke after his visit to China, he highlighted a quiet shift reshaping Europe’s ties with Beijing. He said Germany could not secure long term prosperity with its current work life balance and shorter working hours, not when compared to China’s industrial pace. His words were not just about domestic policy. They showed Germany’s growing worry about its economic position and how its relationship with China had moved far beyond simple cooperation.

2025 marks 50 years of diplomatic relations between the EU and China. Over five decades, this partnership has changed completely. It started as an unequal bond based on shared needs, and it has grown into intense strategic competition that defines Germany’s role as Europe’s economic leader.

In the mid 1970s, West Germany’s export led economy faced trouble after the 1973 oil crisis. Slow growth and crowded Western markets pushed West Germany to see China as a new market for machinery, chemicals and industrial equipment. For China, the partnership served two key aims. China wanted Western technology and investment to support modernisation. It also wanted Western partners to balance the threat from the Soviet Union after the Sino Soviet split.

Formal ties between the EEC and China were settled on 6 May 1975. Premier Zhou Enlai approved the agreement, which included a clear one China commitment. The relationship had a clear imbalance. Europe provided advanced technology and expertise. China offered geopolitical weight and a large labour force.

That imbalance faded over many years. China’s economic reforms turned it into the world’s second largest economy, with a high tech industrial base that competes directly with Europe. By 2016, China became Germany’s largest trading partner. This was the high point of their economic interdependence. The relationship soon changed. Complementary trade turned into competition, and the EU adopted its partner, competitor, rival framework in 2019 to reflect this new reality.

Germany now struggles to protect its own industry while supporting European unity. Formal diplomatic spaces, such as the EU China Trade and Technology Council and Climate Dialogue, aim to create a unified European stance. Germany’s national interests often get in the way of this unity.

The EU’s dispute over electric vehicles shows this split clearly. German officials opposed EU plans to place anti subsidy tariffs on Chinese electric vehicles. They feared Chinese retaliation that would block German car exports to China, the world’s largest car market. The final agreement was a weak compromise. It exposed a weak point in EU policy. European decision making is slow and divided, while China acts with consistency and speed.

Climate cooperation follows a similar pattern. The EU China Climate Dialogue still matters for joint green action, but German concerns hold back progress. China now leads global production of solar panels and key battery parts, pulling ahead of German firms in important green technology areas. Merz has also criticised EU green rules as too much bureaucracy that harms German industrial competitiveness. His push to cut red tape is a direct answer to China’s competitive edge, and it creates more tension within the EU.

Informal ties tell an even more complex story. German political leaders talk openly about competition, but German businesses stay deeply involved with China. Research by Barbora Valocková shows German business leaders are not united. Large multinational companies still earn strong profits from China, while smaller firms face growing pressure from Chinese competitors. This split explains Germany’s two track approach. Business groups avoid confrontation to protect profits, while political leaders raise alarms about industrial decline.

German direct investment in China has fallen since 2016. Companies worry that deeper ties will weaken Germany’s own industrial strength. The rise in investment between 2024 and 2025 was not a sign of renewed confidence. It was a defensive choice to avoid uncertainty from US protectionism. Germany does not have a single, clear China strategy. Its policy comes from competing business and political goals.

Germany and China will keep competing in cars, green technology and global industrial leadership. Full confrontation is not certain, but managed rivalry is the only realistic path forward. Germany must protect its industrial base, strengthen European unity and keep the cooperative spirit of 50 years of relations alive.

Merz’s efforts to lift productivity and cut bureaucracy will shape the future of this relationship. If Germany can simplify rules and regain industrial flexibility, it can compete effectively with China while keeping practical cooperation. The path ahead will have tension, but this balanced and controlled engagement is vital for Germany’s economic and diplomatic future.
