Relations at 50: From Asymmetric Partnership to Managed Rivalry

By Mariam Benamer | 13th April 2026

Introduction

Soon after German Chancellor Friedrich Merz returned from his visit to China, he expressed deep concern for Germany’s economic and industrial future:

"Germany could not secure long-term prosperity with its current work-life balance and shorter working hours, not when compared to China's industrial pace."

While Merz has previously highlighted Germany’s competitive weaknesses and the challenges facing its industrial model, his comments carried renewed weight after direct, first-hand engagement with China. His statement serves as both a warning about Germany’s eroding productivity and a quiet acknowledgment of China’s rapid industrial advancement. Even as Germany maintains formal diplomatic and economic ties with Beijing, this underlying anxiety helps explain the shift from asymmetric partnership to managed rivalry. His assessment reflects a broader realignment. No longer simply partners in trade and growth, Germany and China now operate within a framework of competition, particularly in manufacturing, green technology and global industrial leadership.

This is no casual remark; it is a clear signal of Germany’s shifting strategic outlook, as Europe’s largest economy adapts to a new reality where cooperation coexists with intense systemic competition.

50 Years of EU and China Relations

2025 marks 50 years of diplomatic relations between the EU and China. Over five decades, this partnership has transformed completely, evolving from an unequal bond forged by shared needs into a relationship defined by intense strategic competition that shapes Germany’s role as Europe’s economic leader.

In the mid 1970s, West Germany’s export led economy struggled in the wake of the 1973 oil crisis. Stagnant growth and saturated Western markets pushed West Germany to view China as a vital new market for its machinery, chemicals and industrial equipment. For China, this partnership served two core purposes. Securing Western technology and investment to drive national modernisation, and gaining Western allies to counterbalance the Soviet threat following the Sino-Soviet split.

Formal diplomatic ties between the EEC and China were finalised on 6 May 1975, with Premier Zhou Enlai endorsing the agreement, which included a firm commitment to the one China principle. The relationship was inherently unbalanced. Europe supplied cutting edge technology and expertise, while China offered significant geopolitical leverage and an expansive labour force.

This asymmetry gradually faded over subsequent decades. China’s sweeping economic reforms propelled it to become the world’s second largest economy, building a high tech industrial base that competes head to head with European industries. By 2016, China had become Germany’s largest trading partner, marking the peak of their economic interdependence.

The nature of the relationship soon shifted irrevocably. Complementary trade ties gave way to direct competition, prompting the EU to adopt its partner, competitor, rival framework in 2019 to reflect this new geopolitical and economic reality. Today, Germany faces the delicate task of safeguarding its domestic industries while upholding European unity.

Formal diplomatic platforms, including the EU China Trade and Technology Council and Climate Dialogue, are designed to forge a unified European position, yet Germany’s national interests frequently hinder this cohesion. The EU’s internal dispute over electric vehicles lays bare this division perfectly. German officials pushed back against EU proposals to impose anti subsidy tariffs on Chinese electric vehicles, fearing retaliatory measures that would cut off German car exports to China, the world’s largest automotive market. The final deal amounted to a weak compromise, exposing a critical flaw in EU policy. European decision making is slow and fragmented, while China acts with unwavering consistency and speed.

Climate cooperation follows a similar disjointed pattern. While the EU China Climate Dialogue remains pivotal for joint green initiatives, German economic concerns continue to stall meaningful progress. China now dominates global production of solar panels and key battery components, surging ahead of German enterprises in critical green technology sectors. Merz has also slammed EU green regulations for excessive bureaucracy that undermines German industrial competitiveness. His push to cut red tape is a direct response to China’s competitive advantage, further fuelling internal tensions within the EU.

Informal bilateral ties paint an even more complex picture. German political leaders openly frame China as a strategic competitor, yet German businesses remain deeply embedded in the Chinese market. Research by Barbora Valocková reveals a split among German business leaders. Large multinational corporations still reap substantial profits from China, while small and medium sized firms face mounting pressure from Chinese domestic competitors.

This divide lies at the heart of Germany’s dual track China policy. Business groups avoid confrontation to protect their bottom lines, while political leaders sound the alarm over long term industrial decline. German direct investment in China has declined steadily since 2016, as companies fear deeper ties will erode Germany’s own industrial strength. The modest rebound in investment between 2024 and 2025 was not a vote of renewed confidence, but a defensive move to avoid the uncertainty sparked by US protectionist policies.

Germany lacks a single, cohesive China strategy. Its policy is instead a product of clashing business and political priorities. Going forward, Germany and China will remain locked in competition across automotive manufacturing, green technology and global industrial leadership. Full scale confrontation is unlikely, but managed rivalry stands as the only realistic path forward.

Conclusion

Germany must protect its industrial base, strengthen European unity and keep the cooperative spirit of 50 years of relations alive. Merz’s efforts to lift productivity and cut bureaucracy will shape the future of this relationship. If Germany can simplify rules and regain industrial flexibility, it can compete effectively with China while keeping practical cooperation. The path ahead will have tension, but this balanced and controlled engagement is vital for Germany’s economic and diplomatic future.
