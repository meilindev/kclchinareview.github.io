Should Xi Jinping stop going on about national humiliation?

By Hannah Zou | 27th February 2026

Introduction

“Forget not national humiliation and become stronger (勿忘国耻，奋发图强 wu wang guo chi, fen fa tu qiang).” This was the official rhetoric deployed by Chinese President Xi Jinping in reference to China’s century of humiliation, a deep colonial wounding from 1839 to 1945. Although the concept of national humiliation is not new, Xi has enshrined it in the state constitution in 2018 (Wei & Hu, 2018), elevating its role in the Chinese Communist Party (CCP)’s historical storytelling. National humiliation rhetoric pervades Xi’s speeches and official propaganda, tied closely to his Great Rejuvenation project. I argue that Xi should continue using the trope of national humiliation for two reasons: it is a critical concept employed by Xi and the CCP, and that national humiliation is useful in cementing Xi’s, the CCP’s, and China’s legitimacy. In part one, I reference Callahan (2023)’s three-dimensional framework of socialism, tradition, and nation. Rather than agreeing with Callahan about how these three narratives intersect to form a composite ideology, I advance that it is the narrative of the Nation that is ultimately supported by the Socialism and Tradition narratives. In part two, I examine whether the trope of national humiliation is useful to Xi and China through two conditions: whether the usage of national humiliation tropes helps to firstly solidify Xi’s and the CCP’s legitimacy and secondly help China to gain its legitimacy in the international order. Finally, in part three, I will explain that although negative effects may arise from the usage of national humiliation rhetoric, it may offer China observers beneficial insight into Chinese foreign policy behaviour.
