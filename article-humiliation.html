Should Xi Jinping stop going on about national humiliation?

By Hannah Zou | 27th February 2026

Introduction

“Forget not national humiliation and become stronger (勿忘国耻，奋发图强 wu wang guo chi, fen fa tu qiang).” This was the official rhetoric deployed by Chinese President Xi Jinping in reference to China’s century of humiliation, a deep colonial wounding from 1839 to 1945. Although the concept of national humiliation is not new, Xi has enshrined it in the state constitution in 2018 (Wei & Hu, 2018), elevating its role in the Chinese Communist Party (CCP)’s historical storytelling. National humiliation rhetoric pervades Xi’s speeches and official propaganda, tied closely to his Great Rejuvenation project.

I argue that Xi should continue using the trope of national humiliation for two reasons: it is a critical concept employed by Xi and the CCP, and that national humiliation is useful in cementing Xi’s, the CCP’s, and China’s legitimacy.

In part one, I reference Callahan (2023)’s three-dimensional framework of socialism, tradition, and nation. Rather than agreeing with Callahan about how these three narratives intersect to form a composite ideology, I advance that it is the narrative of the Nation that is ultimately supported by the Socialism and Tradition narratives. In part two, I examine whether the trope of national humiliation is useful to Xi and China through two conditions: whether the usage of national humiliation tropes helps to firstly solidify Xi’s and the CCP’s legitimacy and secondly help China to gain its legitimacy in the international order. Finally, in part three, I will explain that although negative effects may arise from the usage of national humiliation rhetoric, it may offer China observers beneficial insight into Chinese foreign policy behaviour.

Key Arguments

Part One: Literature Review

In Chinese Global Orders: Socialism, Tradition, and Nation in China–Russia Relations (ibid.), Callahan introduces three main Chinese approaches to the international order: Socialism, Tradition, and Nation. He argues that they intersect to form a composite ideology of neo-socialism (ibid., p.3).

Callahan writes that the Socialism narrative is present in the CCP’s Marxist ideology and concept of United Front Work. Indeed, Xi has described Marx as “a teacher of revolution … and the greatest thinker of modern times” (Xinhua, 2018). Xi also emphasised that it is “only through socialism can we save China”. Callahan explains that Xi sees United Front work “as one of the CCP’s three ‘magic weapons’” (Callahan, 2023, p.6) to carry on with the fight for socialism to achieve “ideological victory” (ibid., p.8).

Callahan notes that the Tradition narrative re-emerges in the CCP’s “neo-socialist understanding of politics and the world,” such as tianxia, a system of concentric circles with China as the centre. Noticeably, there is a recurrent concept of Sinocentrism in the narrative of Tradition. Such a narrative positions the party as the central leader of a new global order to achieve Great Harmony Under Heaven.

Finally, Callahan explores the narrative of the Nation with a focus on the CCP’s historiography of national humiliation and the correction of China’s century of humiliation through national rejuvenation. Callahan explains that the CCP’s version of modern Chinese history writes that “China is destined to rejuvenate and reclaim its rightful place at the centre of global civilisation”, most evident in the ‘China Dream of national rejuvenation’ (ibid., p.16). The key ideas of national humiliation and national rejuvenation are closely linked, with the former being ‘the problem’ of past exploitation and the latter being ‘the solution’ to achieve future prestige. Xi enunciates that “only by adhering to and developing socialism with Chinese characteristics can [China]realize national rejuvenation” (Xinhua, 2018), underlining thatnational rejuvenation is deeply entwined with Xi’s rhetoric and is necessary for the recovery from national humiliation.

I disagree with Callahan’s argument that these three narratives intersect and form a composite ideology. Instead, I advance that it is the narrative of the Nation that is ultimately supported by the Socialism and Tradition narratives. I caution against thinking that the Nation is the only important narrative; rather, I purport that it is the Nation that is the most important of the three narratives. The Nation narrative, and therefore national humiliation, is a deeply foundational gateway for China’s national rejuvenation.

To illustrate how Socialism reinforces the Nation narrative, references to socialism are co-opted into Chinese nationalist rhetoric through the incorporation of China-specific vocabulary. An instance of this is the term “Socialism with Chinese Characteristics”, in which the notion of socialism is more explicitly intertwined with distinct Chinese features. Moreover, in 2019, Xi emphasised that “’ Socialism with Chinese Characteristics’… is imbued with characteristically Chinese features bestowed by the conditions of the times” (Greer, 2019; Qiushiwang, 2019), further deepening the linkage between socialism and uniquely Chinese features. It is also repeatedly reinforced across Xi’s speeches that there is a need to build a China into a modern socialist country to advance the rejuvenation of China (Zhonghuarenmingongheguo Waijiaobu, 2023; China Neican, 2023; Council on Foreign Relations, 2022). Therefore, we can observe that socialism is now the ideological driver of China’s rejuvenation, a key concept of the Nation narrative.

Similarly, Tradition is now being utilised to justify national rejuvenation, underpinning the narrative of the Nation. Xi has repeatedly stated that China aims to establish a new international order with new values upon national rejuvenation. Particularly, Xi has encouraged China to assume the leading role of a reformed global order and contribute positively to the reconstruction of the new world order (Liao Wang, 2023). This is articulated by official slogans such as the Community with a Shared Future for Mankind, which places China in the centre of the world in a manner reminiscent of tianxia. As observed, the narrative of Tradition legitimises a China-centric world order and, in turn, justifies national rejuvenation, a core element of the Nation narrative.

Overall, I have proposed a hierarchy among these narratives to highlight the importance of the Nation narrative. As national humiliation remains central to Xi’s discourse and legitimises the other narratives, he should continue using it.

Part Two: Assessing the utility of the national humiliation rhetoric

To deduce whether Xi should stop going on about national humiliation, I will examine whether the trope of national humiliation is useful to Xi and China through two conditions: whether it solidifies Xi’s and the CCP’s legitimacy in the eyes of the Chinese citizenry, and whether it helps China to gain legitimacy in the world. If both conditions are met, we can conclude that Xi should continue his usage of the national humiliation trope.

Condition 1: Solidifying Xi’s and the CCP’s legitimacy

Modern Chinese political legitimacy rests on how far leaders can save China from its century of humiliation and re-transform it into a powerful nation. As Kaufman (2015) puts it, such an imperative “has become a touchstone for each leader’s articulation of China’s national aspirations, and a justification for the path that each leader says China should take to achieve those aspirations.” As such, Xi has “inherited the mandate to rejuvenate China” (ibid.). By making the great rejuvenation of the Chinese nation his core ideological project, Xi ties it to his own slogans, turning the loyalty to that mission into personal loyalty to him. Designated as the Core of the Party (Renmin Ribao, 2017), Xi is now presented as the central leader whose authority is necessary to avoid a return to past national humiliation. Deputy director of the Party-building department at the Party School of the CCP Central Committee Dai Yanjun emphasised that Xi’s core status was “established through his leadership in advancing the Party’s great causes” (Xinhua, 2016), one which includes the concept of national rejuvenation. From the same Party school, chief expert on Marxist studies Yan Shuhan stated that Xi being the core is the fundamental interests of the nation,” as China is now “closer to its goal of great rejuvenation than at any point in history” (ibid.). Since Xi has successfully used the concept of national rejuvenation emerging from national humiliation to solidify his legitimacy within the Party, national humiliation is a valuable trope he should continue to deploy.

On the party level, the national humiliation trope is a key legitimiser for CCP rule. By employing the victim narrative, the CCP portrays itself as “the only modern Chinese political party that was able to successfully stand up to foreign aggression” and put an end to national humiliation (Liu, 2009). This portrayal is most clearly seen in the Patriotic Education Campaign in the 1990s, where instead of focusing on China’s suffering due to the actions of foreign powers, it spotlighted the important role the CCP played in leading China out of its bitter suffering (Wang, 2008). For instance, in 1993, the State Commission of Education directed the distribution of historical films depicting China’s War of Resistance against Japanese Aggression to all educational institutions in China (Kotsonis, 2017). Furthermore, Xi’s usage of national humiliation drives home the point of “what can happen without strong CCP leadership” (Council on Foreign Relations, 2025). By reinforcing the pains that the Chinese had experienced by constantly recounting history from the perspective of a victim, the Chinese citizenry may be stirred to support the Party in hopes of overcoming past humiliation (Charoenmuang, 2022). Hence, by relying on the narrative of national humiliation, the CCP can solidify its legitimacy in the eyes of the Chinese citizenry.

Moreover, the national humiliation rhetoric rallies the people and drums up popular support, further strengthening the legitimacy of the CCP. By selectively representing events in history and reconstructing historical memories, the Party can wield national humiliation as a mobilisation tool (Wang, 2020) during external conflicts. By reconstructing China’s historiography, the Party forms a collective trauma that drives the nation towards “recapturing glory in a redeemed future” (Brown, 2018). Again, we see the slogan of national rejuvenation built on national humiliation to rouse the citizenry in Xi’s speech at the 70th anniversary of the founding of Modern China: “…no force can stop the Chinese people and the Chinese nation forging ahead” (CCTV Video News Agency, 2019). Since the CCP uses the national humiliation rhetoric to solidify its legitimacy by consolidating support, it is a strategic device that should continue to be deployed.

Condition 2: Cementing China’s legitimacy in the world

National humiliation is also used in foreign policy to fulfil various Chinese core interests. Core interests include sovereignty, territorial integrity, and sustainable economic development (Guowuyuan Xinwen Bangongshi, 2025). I show how national humiliation can be used as a foreign policy instrument to drum up domestic nationalism and justify China’s economic statecraft.
